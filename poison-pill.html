03 · The shield

The most elegant defense in corporate law.

A poison pill does not stop anyone from buying shares. It makes buying too many of them ruinously expensive, by threatening to flood the market with cheap new stock the moment a bidder crosses a line.

First principles

How a shareholder rights plan works

A poison pill, known formally as a shareholder rights agreement, is a contract a board can adopt on its own, without a shareholder vote. It distributes a "right" to every existing share. The right lies dormant and worthless until a single investor accumulates more than a set threshold of the company, commonly 10% to 20%, without board blessing.

Cross that line and the trigger flips. Every shareholder except the bidder is suddenly allowed to buy additional shares at a steep discount, often half price. The flood of cheap new stock dilutes the bidder's stake dramatically and makes the next share far more costly to acquire. No rational acquirer trips the pill on purpose. The point is not to fire it; the point is that it exists, which forces a would-be buyer to negotiate with the board rather than go around it.

Trigger

Cross the line

A bidder passes the ownership threshold without approval.

Dilution

Everyone else buys cheap

Other holders exercise rights at a discount, swamping the bidder.

Effect

Talk to the board

The bidder must negotiate, or win the board through a proxy vote.

The Genco pill

A defense built before the attack

Timing is the tell. Genco adopted its rights agreement on October 1, 2025, after Diana had begun accumulating stock but before Diana made its first written proposal in November.1 The plan set a 15% trigger, and Diana responded by holding its position just beneath that level, building to roughly 14% so as not to set it off.2

The pill did exactly what pills are designed to do. It removed any path for Diana to creep to control through open-market buying and forced the contest onto two tracks the board could fight: a conditional tender that needed the board's own cooperation to close, and a proxy campaign to replace directors. When shareholders ratified the rights plan at the June 18, 2026 annual meeting, the defense hardened further, this time with an explicit mandate from the owners.3

The family knows this weapon well

Genco's defense is close to the one a Palios company used to survive its own hostile suitor. The difference is structural: Performance Shipping was protected by a family super-voting block as well as a pill, so it was mathematically unassailable. Genco has no controlling holder, so its pill had to be backed by an actual shareholder vote, which it won. See the Performance page.
