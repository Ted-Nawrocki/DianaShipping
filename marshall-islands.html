05 · The flag

A New York deal, governed from a Pacific atoll by way of Virginia.

Diana, Genco, Performance, OceanPal, the family's holding vehicles, and almost every ship in these fleets share one legal home: the Republic of the Marshall Islands. It is not a footnote. It shapes how this fight can be fought.

The jurisdiction

Sovereign nation, American legal genes

The Marshall Islands is a sovereign Pacific country, independent since its Compact of Free Association with the United States entered into force in 1986.1 Its corporate framework, the Business Corporations Act of 1990, is modeled closely on Delaware's corporate law, which is precisely why American lawyers, lenders and capital-markets participants are comfortable with it. A US attorney reading a Marshall Islands charter recognizes the structure immediately.2

The registry that administers all of this is not in the Pacific at all. Both the maritime and corporate registries are run by International Registries, Inc., a private company headquartered in Reston, Virginia, with offices in dozens of maritime centers worldwide.3 For non-resident corporations, income earned outside the Republic is untaxed, which is one reason the structure is the default for international shipping ownership.2

Why it matters here

Delaware's rules, without Delaware's case law

The Marshall Islands borrowed Delaware's statute but not its vast body of judicial precedent. The country's own courts have decided comparatively few corporate-control disputes, so the detailed, decades-deep framework that governs hostile takeovers of US-domiciled companies, the line of cases that polices defensive tactics and board conduct in a sale, does not bind a Marshall Islands board in the same way. In practice that gives boards wide latitude to adopt and keep defensive measures such as poison pills and super-voting share classes.

This is the quiet structural fact underneath the whole saga. It is why a Palios company could maintain a super-voting block at Performance with little fear of being unwound by a court, and why Genco's board can run its rights plan with confidence. It is also why, when George Economou's New York suit against the Performance directors was dismissed for lack of personal jurisdiction, the natural next venue was the Marshall Islands itself, where the companies and the family's holding vehicles are all incorporated and therefore subject to suit.5

On the water, too

The flag is not only on the share registers. Vessel after vessel in the Diana fleet is documented under the Marshall Islands flag, the registry's standard offering for dry bulk and tanker owners, alongside foreign-maritime-entity registration that lets even non-RMI companies fly it.6 The corporate domicile and the flag state are, deliberately, the same place.
