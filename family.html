02 · The Family

One family, several flags

Map the personal holding vehicles and a pattern appears: this is a family that engineered a different control mechanism for each of its three listed companies, calibrated to how much of the equity it actually owns.

The patriarch

Simeon Palios is the origin point. He has worked in shipping since 1969, trained as a naval architect and marine engineer, holds a degree from Durham University, and served as an ensign in the Greek Navy inspecting passenger boats for the Ministry of Merchant Marine.1 He founded Diana Shipping Agencies S.A. and ran it as managing director until November 2004, building over his career a group that controlled more than fifty ships across dry bulk, containers, and tankers. He remains Diana's chairman, and since 2015 has been president of the Friends of Biomedical Research Foundation, Academy of Athens. He is sometimes rendered publicly as Simos Palios.

Two daughters, two companies, one deliberate split

The succession of 2020 cleaved the group cleanly along family lines, and the two sisters could hardly be more different in training.

Semiramis Paliou is the operator-engineer. She holds a BSc in Mechanical Engineering from Imperial College London and an MSc in Naval Architecture from University College London, began as a trainee ship surveyor at Lloyd's Register from 1996 to 1998, and has worked in the group since 1998.1 She became Diana's chief executive in March 2021 and also chaired OceanPal through October 2025.

Aliki Paliou came from an entirely different world. She studied Theatre Studies at the University of Kent, took an MA in Scenography at Central Saint Martins in London, and graduated from the Greek School of Fine Art in Athens in 2005.2 She chairs Performance Shipping and is married to its chief executive, Andreas Michalopoulos.

The mechanics of the split were precise. At Performance's February 2020 board meeting the company accepted the resignations of Semiramis Paliou as chief operating officer, Ioannis Zafirakis as chief strategy officer and secretary, and Anastasios Margaronis as president, all of whom moved to Diana, while Aliki Paliou took a board seat at Performance. The senior professional lieutenants went to run the dry bulk parent; Aliki and her husband took the tanker vehicle.

The web of personal holding vehicles

Each principal holds their stake through a private Marshall Islands entity rather than in their own name, and those entities are the actual instruments of control.

At Performance, Aliki Paliou owns and controls Mango Shipping Corp., which holds Series C preferred convertible into 24,268,863 common shares, while Andreas Michalopoulos owns and controls Mitzela Corp., holding Series C preferred convertible into 1,039,979 common shares. Each Series C share converts on a 25.00 dollar preference over a 1.3576 dollar conversion price, with no expiration date.3

At OceanPal, Semiramis Paliou runs an even more elaborate structure. Through Tuscany Shipping Corp. and 4 Sweet Dreams S.A., both of which she controls entirely, she holds Series C and Series D convertible preferred amounting to 9,718,898 common-equivalent shares, about 34 percent of the class, and she also holds 1,200 super-voting Series E preferred shares granting up to 25,000 votes each, capped at 15 percent of total votes. She holds no common stock directly.4

Three companies, three control designs

The strategically interesting fact is that the family owns very different amounts of each company, and has bolted on exactly as much voting machinery as each situation required.

Family control architecture across the three listed vehicles
CompanyFamily economic ownershipControl mechanism
Diana (NYSE: DSX) Anchor stake, reported in the low-to-mid 20 percent range One share, one vote. No super-voting class. Influence rests on ownership plus board presence.
Performance (Nasdaq: PSHG) Modest common, but roughly 90 percent of the votes Series C super-voting preferred carrying ten votes per share, held through Mango and Mitzela.
OceanPal / SovereignAI (Nasdaq: SVRN) About 34 percent via convertibles Two layers: Diana the company holds super-voting preferred for up to 34 percent of votes, and Semiramis personally holds Series E super-voting on top.

Diana is the one vehicle where the family does not hold a voting lock. That single fact explains a great deal about the Genco campaign: a family that wins control fights through structure walked into a target where it had only a roughly 14 percent stake and no super-voting class to fall back on.

The operating plumbing and the rotating lieutenants

Underneath the listed companies sits a set of related-party management entities, all family-affiliated: Diana Shipping Agencies S.A. (1972), Diana Shipping Services S.A. (formed 1986), Performance Shipping Management Inc., and Steamship Shipbroking Enterprises Inc., which co-manages OceanPal's fleet alongside Diana Wilhelmsen Management Limited, Diana's fifty-fifty technical management joint venture with Wilhelmsen Ship Management.

Two non-family executives are the connective tissue. Ioannis Zafirakis, now Diana's president, spent 2010 to 2020 as a director and senior executive of Performance, then Diana Containerships, and Anastasios Margaronis, a Diana director and former Performance president, personally holds about 7.57 percent of OceanPal. The same small group of people has sat on multiple sides of these vehicles over time, which is part of why the family can move quickly across companies.
