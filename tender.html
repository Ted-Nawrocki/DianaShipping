02 · The deal · as of June 27 2026

A bear hug that turned into a siege.

Diana ran the classic three-pronged hostile playbook: a private approach, then an unsolicited tender offer, then a proxy fight. Genco refused to engage at every step, and the shareholders backed the board.

The shape of it

Four facts that frame the fight

The acquirer is the smaller one

Diana's equity was worth about $206 million in late 2025; the Genco offer values the target's equity near $1.0 to $1.2 billion. Diana is bridging the gap with $1.433 billion of committed bank financing and no financing condition.1

A partner is waiting

Star Bulk Carriers has agreed to buy sixteen Genco vessels for $470.5 million on completion, monetizing nearly half a billion dollars at closing and signaling a coordinated consolidation play.1

The tender is not really a tender

It is conditioned on a signed merger agreement and removal of Genco's poison pill, neither of which can happen without board cooperation. Tendering shares closes nothing, which is why almost none were tendered.2

The ballot has spoken

At the June 18 2026 annual meeting, on average nearly 90% of non-Diana shares backed Genco's directors and rejected Diana's two nominees, and shareholders ratified the rights plan.3

The offer ladder

How the price climbed, and the engagement did not

  1. July 17 2025

    Diana files a 13D

    Diana discloses an accumulated position in Genco, building toward roughly 14% and signaling strategic intent.2

  2. October 1 2025

    Genco arms the defense

    Ahead of any formal bid, Genco adopts a shareholder rights plan with a 15% trigger. Diana parks its stake just below it.4

  3. November 24 2025

    $20.60 per share

    The first written proposal. Genco declines to engage.2

  4. March 6 2026

    $23.50 per share

    A raised proposal, again rejected without negotiation.2

  5. May 4 2026

    The tender goes live at $23.50

    Diana takes the offer directly to shareholders, conditioned on a merger agreement and removal of the pill.2

  6. May 27 2026

    $24.80, and a proxy slate

    Diana raises the cash price and extends the deadline while pressing board nominees. As of this date, fewer than 40 shares had been tendered.2

  7. June 17 2026

    $27.34: cash plus a Diana share

    The restructured offer adds equity, $24.80 in cash plus one Diana share, valued at about $27.34, made in partnership with Star Bulk and conditioned on a registration statement. It is non-binding.5

  8. June 18 2026

    The proxy rout

    Genco shareholders back the incumbent board decisively and ratify the rights plan.3

  9. June 26 2026

    The tender expires

    The revised tender was set to lapse at 5:00 p.m. New York time. As of June 27, the result was not yet public; Diana committed to announce any extension by the next business morning.5

The argument

The fight is about net asset value, at the top of the cycle

Diana's case

Dry bulk is valued off the steel. Diana frames its bids at roughly net asset value on broker marks and notes that Genco has historically traded at an average 30% discount to NAV, so a cash deal at NAV hands shareholders that discount today.2 It adds that take-private shipping deals have on average closed at a discount to NAV, not a premium.

Genco's case

Genco points to higher sell-side NAV estimates, a mean near $26.66 and median near $27.10, and demands a control premium on top.6 Its financial and legal advisers delivered inadequacy opinions, and the board has framed the conditional tender as uncertain. At one point it suggested it had the stronger balance sheet and should be the buyer.

The banker's read

Asset values sit at or near fifteen-year highs, so NAV itself is peaky. A buyer paying about one times a cyclical-top NAV can credibly call that full; a target demanding a premium above the top is asking the buyer to pay through the cycle. Add a small-cap acquirer using leverage and a partner's asset purchase to swallow a larger peer, and the tension is structural, not cosmetic.
