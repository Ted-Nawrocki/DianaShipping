Before the family ran a siege, it survived one.

From 2023 to 2026, a Palios company was the target of an almost identical attack to the one Diana now presses on Genco. How the family defended Performance Shipping explains a great deal about how it thinks.

From container ships to Aframax tankers

Performance Shipping was formerly Diana Containerships Inc., spun out of the Diana group and listed on Nasdaq under the ticker PSHG.1 Under Aliki Paliou as chair and her husband Andreas Michalopoulos as chief executive, the company shed its container fleet and rebuilt around Aframax crude tankers, later adding LR2 and Suezmax tonnage.1 Its registered manager, Performance Shipping Management Inc., sits at the same Athens address as the wider group. During 2022 and 2023 the company carried out a series of equity raises and built a defensive capital structure on top of them, the structure that would soon be the center of a fight.

George Economou comes knocking

  1. August 2023
    A stake appears. George Economou, the shipping financier behind DryShips, builds a roughly 9 percent position in Performance through his vehicle Sphinx Investment Corp.2
  2. 11 October 2023
    A 3.00 dollar cash tender. Sphinx launches a hostile tender for all common stock at 3.00 dollars per share, a stated premium of about 78.6 percent to the prior close. Most of its conditions sat within the board's own control, including cancellation of the company's super-voting Series C preferred.2
  3. Late 2023
    A proxy fight and a lawsuit. Economou seeks a board seat, the removal of four directors, and board declassification, nominating a slate. His counsel files suit in New York alleging that the directors and family insiders breached fiduciary duties by creating a dual-class structure that handed control to the family.3
  4. 2024
    Dismissed on jurisdiction, refiled. The New York court dismisses the complaint solely because it lacked personal jurisdiction over the defendants, without reaching the merits. A substantially similar suit is then filed in the Marshall Islands, where the relevant entities are incorporated.4
  5. March 2026
    The tender is terminated. After many extensions across more than two years, Sphinx terminates its tender offer. The family's control had never been in mathematical jeopardy.5

What the complaint alleged, and what is established

The contested claims here come from a filed pleading, and they are presented as allegations, not findings. According to the complaint, the family used an exchange offer that let common holders swap into non-voting Series B preferred they could not sell, later convertible into Series C preferred carrying ten times the voting power of common stock, with the effect that the family came to control roughly 90 percent of the votes through the vehicles Mango and Mitzela.3 The complaint characterized this as a self-dealing scheme that disenfranchised common shareholders.

Performance's board disputed those characterizations and recommended that shareholders reject the tender and not tender their shares.6 The New York case was dismissed on jurisdiction without any ruling on whether the allegations were true.4 What is firmly established from SEC filings is the ownership architecture itself: the existence of the Series C super-voting preferred and the Mango and Mitzela holding vehicles.7 The motive ascribed to it remains an unproven allegation.

A second front

Economou also pressed OceanPal, another Palios-controlled vehicle, building toward roughly 14 percent and demanding the removal of most of its board. That one resolved commercially: in mid-2024 he stood down under a support agreement. OceanPal's subsequent annual materials quantified the related support-agreement cost at 6.75 million dollars.8

Defender then, aggressor now

Set the two fights side by side and the symmetry is striking. At Performance, the family ran the defender's script: appoint a committee, refuse to engage, call the conditional tender illusory, keep the defensive structures, and outlast the bidder. At Genco, Diana now makes the attacker's arguments Economou once aimed at the family, that the board is entrenched, refuses to engage, and has erected defenses to protect management.

The decisive difference is the control block. Performance was unbreakable because the family held about 90 percent of the votes. Genco has no controlling holder, and Diana owns only about 14 percent, so the only path was to win an actual shareholder vote. A family that wins these fights through structure walked into a target where structure was not available, and lost the ballot. See the bid and the pill.
