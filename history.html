01 · Dynasty

Diana and its offspring

One Athens ship agency, founded in 1972, eventually produced three public companies, two hostile takeover fights, and a crypto-treasury detour. This is how the house was built.

From a Chios family trade to a New York listing

The Palios shipping activity began in 1972, when Simeon Palios established Diana Shipping Agencies S.A. in Athens and ran it as managing director until November 2004.1 The group's own management materials trace its lineage further back still, to a ship-management house that, by the early twentieth century, handled much of the fleet linking the Greek islands with the mainland.2

The listed company came later. Diana Shipping Inc. traces to a 1999 incorporation, was renamed from Diana Shipping Investments Corp. in February 2005, and listed on the New York Stock Exchange in March 2005, raising roughly 217 million dollars in net proceeds earmarked for vessel acquisitions.2 Like most of the large Greek owners, the company is run from Athens but incorporated under the flag of the Marshall Islands, a structural choice that becomes important once a takeover fight reaches the courts.

The house style was set early and has held: modern tonnage employed on fixed-rate period charters, low leverage, and predictable quarterly cash flow rather than spot-market bets. That conservatism is the backdrop against which the family's more adventurous moves stand out.

Two spin-offs, two very different paths

Rather than run a single platform, the family has repeatedly used the public markets to spin off and repurpose vehicles. Two spin-offs matter here.

Performance Shipping

The first was Diana Containerships Inc. (Nasdaq: PSHG). After the 2020 succession, Simeon's daughter Aliki Paliou renamed it Performance Shipping and steered it out of container ships and into Aframax tankers, a clean break from the dry bulk parent.3 Performance is the company that, three years later, would find itself on the receiving end of a hostile bid from George Economou. That story has its own page.

OceanPal

The second was OceanPal. On November 29, 2021, Diana distributed one OceanPal share for every ten Diana shares and seeded the new company with three of its oldest vessels, listing it on Nasdaq under the symbol OP.4 OceanPal is run by Semiramis Paliou, the same executive now leading the Genco bid, and the parent retained voting control through super-voting preferred stock. In practice it has behaved less like an independent company than a dropdown vehicle for the group's older ships.

The strangest chapter

OceanPal becomes SovereignAI

In the second half of 2025 OceanPal pivoted hard away from shipping. It ran a reverse stock split effective August 25, 2025, then on October 28, 2025 announced a 120 million dollar PIPE with the NEAR Foundation to launch a subsidiary called SovereignAI built around a crypto treasury strategy.5 By November 13, 2025 the subsidiary reported holding 53.9 million NEAR tokens worth roughly 133 million dollars, and the company changed its Nasdaq ticker from OP to SVRN effective November 17, 2025.5 It later received a Nasdaq delinquency notice in May 2026. A dry bulk spin-off had, within four years, become a digital-asset treasury vehicle while still owning a small mixed maritime fleet.

The 2020 succession

Simeon Palios began stepping back in 2020 and the handover was precise rather than gradual. He served as Diana's chief executive from February 2005 until February 2021, then relinquished the CEO role to his daughter Semiramis Paliou in March 2021 while keeping the chairmanship.1 Ioannis Zafirakis, a director since 2005 and a longtime senior executive, became Diana's president in January 2026.1 The succession split the group along family lines: Semiramis took the dry bulk parent, while Aliki and her husband Andreas Michalopoulos took the tanker vehicle. The full cast, and the holding companies behind each name, sit on The Family page.

The conservative house turns acquirer

For most of its public life Diana was known for what it did not do: it did not chase spot rates, did not over-leverage, and did not make splashy acquisitions. The Genco campaign broke that pattern. Beginning in November 2025, Diana, already Genco's largest shareholder, launched a series of escalating proposals for the largest United States headquartered dry bulk owner, eventually pairing a cash tender with a proxy contest. The mechanics, the offer ladder, and the outcome are laid out on The Bid.

The throughline worth carrying into the rest of this dossier is the contrast. The same family that spent 2023 to 2026 defending Performance against a hostile bidder, using super-voting stock to make itself unassailable, turned around and played the aggressor at Genco, where it had no such control block to rely on.

A note on the name

The public record does not explain why Simeon Palios chose the name Diana. The company's own filings, the founder's interviews, and the trade-press profiles are all silent on it, and the name attaches to the 1972 agency rather than the listed company. Greek owners commonly name a house after a first vessel, a female family member, a patron saint, or a classical figure, but no source ties Diana to any specific person or ship, so this dossier does not assert one.
